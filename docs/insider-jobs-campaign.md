## The fact that changes everything

LinkedIn publishes a rulebook for the third-party systems that feed jobs into it. It lives on Microsoft's documentation site. Guideline 18 says, verbatim:

> "Beginning in August 2024, certain third-party jobs **may no longer be visible on LinkedIn** when they are ingested as Basic Jobs or Limited Listings from third-party sources such as applicant tracking systems (ATSs) and job boards… Please note that **LinkedIn reserves the right to require promotion of jobs.**"

Read it twice. A company opens a real job in its own hiring system. The job moves toward LinkedIn. It may simply never appear — and LinkedIn reserves the right to require the company to pay before you are allowed to see it.

The same rulebook orders third-party feeds to exclude **closed and expired listings** (guideline 4), exclude **duplicates** (guideline 3), and exclude **jobs aggregated from other job boards** (guideline 6). A platform does not write those rules unless stale, duplicated, re-aggregated listings keep arriving.

**This replaces every statistic we were tempted to use.** It is the competitor's own signature. See `source-advantage-citations.md` for the full register.

> **Pivot the story from latency to visibility.** "You were late" invites the reply *"fine, I'll check more often."* **"You were never shown the job"** has no reply at all.

## The idea, in plain terms

A company opens a role inside its own hiring system. That system is what the company's own careers page reads from. It is the original record of the job.

The big job boards get a *copy* — later, and only if the company bothered. Listing a role on a major board costs money per slot. Plenty of companies skip it, hire from referrals, or never get around to it. A job board shows you what a company chose to syndicate. Nothing more.

Three things follow, and all three are true without a single statistic:

1. **Ghost jobs.** Close a role in a company's own system and it disappears that instant. On the boards, the listing can sit there for months, collecting applications nobody reads. Plus duplicates, staffing-agency reposts of the same role, and listings kept alive to farm résumés.
2. **You're always late.** Aggregators refresh on a crawl schedule. The original changes the moment a role opens.
3. **Nobody joins the two halves.** Job boards show you jobs. Your professional network lives in a different product on a different screen. Nothing puts them together.

CareerIQ does the third thing. It finds the companies where you already know someone, reads those companies' hiring systems directly, and hands you the open role **and** the person who can walk it in — in the same row.

That is what *insider* means here. Not another feed. A warm path with a live posting attached.

## Why it lands

The executive we serve has been applying into a void. They have concluded that the market rejected them, or that their age did.

Often they were applying to stale copies of roles that were already filled, duplicated, or never truly open — through the one channel where they are always applicant number 300.

Naming that is a **relief, not an insult.** It reframes months of silence as a channel problem instead of a competence problem. Write to the person who thinks the problem is them.

## Rules — non-negotiable

**Never name the hiring systems.** No vendor names, no platform count, no company-coverage count, no explanation of how a company is resolved to its board. That is the moat and it stays inside the building. Say *"the company's own hiring system," "the source," "where the job is actually born."* A reader should finish the ad wanting the capability and unable to rebuild it.

**Quote LinkedIn's guidelines freely. Everything else numeric is banned.**

| Banned | Why | Say instead |
|---|---|---|
| "70–80% of jobs are never advertised" | Traces to a 1966 article about one city where large employers had agreed to use a local placement service instead of newspapers. The signature stat of resume mills — and we are the brand that says *no invented metrics*. | LinkedIn's own words: certain jobs from company hiring systems "may no longer be visible on LinkedIn." |
| "The boards post 20M+ jobs a day" | False. That's roughly the total number of *live listings*, not a daily rate. | "The boards are enormous. Enormous, and full of copies." |
| Any latency interval — "24–48 hours," "1–3 day head start," "30% of roles 5 days late" | Every trail leads to a vendor selling a job-monitoring product, self-published, no methodology. We haven't measured it. | "By the time it reaches the boards, it's old news inside the company." Direction, not interval. |
| Applicant-rank tables; "recruiters screen the first 25 at 3–5× the rate" | Same vendor blogs. Unaudited. Don't launder someone else's unsourced numbers through our brand. | "Being early matters." Or cite TalentWorks *with attribution*. |

**The one number you may use, with attribution:** one widely cited 2017 analysis of ~1,600 applications (TalentWorks) found applicants were up to 8× more likely to get an interview when applying within the first 96 hours. Write *"one widely cited analysis found…"* — never *"studies show,"* never as our data.

**Never name LinkedIn in an ad that runs on LinkedIn** — their ad policies prohibit disparaging the platform. Use *"the big job boards."* On Meta and Google, employment ads fall under the Special Ad Category: keep every claim about the *mechanism*, never about outcomes, and let the copy self-select its audience.
