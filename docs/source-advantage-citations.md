# Source Advantage — approved citation register

Every factual claim in Source Advantage / Insider Jobs content must trace to this page. Verified 2026-07-09.

Three tiers. **Tier 1 is quotable as fact.** Tier 2 needs attribution in the copy itself. Tier 3 never appears in anything we publish.

---

## Tier 1 — quotable as fact

### LinkedIn's own Third-Party Job Listing and Job Site Inclusion Guidelines

Published by LinkedIn on Microsoft Learn. Doc last updated 2025-11-18.
<https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/linkedin/talent/job-postings/xml-feeds-job-posting-guidelines>

This is the spine of the entire campaign. It is LinkedIn describing LinkedIn, in writing, on Microsoft's own documentation site. It cannot be dismissed as our marketing, and it cannot be answered.

**The best fact we have — pay-to-appear / invisibility.** Guideline 18, verbatim:

> "Beginning in August 2024, certain third-party jobs may no longer be visible on LinkedIn when they are ingested as Basic Jobs or Limited Listings from third-party sources such as applicant tracking systems (ATSs) and job boards. These limits are designed to prevent abuse, improve product quality, and increase transparency while ensuring that job seekers have access to the best possible opportunities. Please note that LinkedIn reserves the right to require promotion of jobs."

A company opens a real role in its own hiring system. The role flows toward LinkedIn. It may never appear — and LinkedIn reserves the right to require the company to pay to promote it.

**Staffing-agency listings.** Guideline 2, verbatim:

> "If you are a staffing agency or recruiter posting jobs on behalf of clients where the employer name cannot be disclosed, these listings are not eligible for Basic Jobs or Limited Listings XML feeds and will require paid promotion on LinkedIn."

**Stale roles.** Guideline 4, verbatim: *"The job feed must not contain closed or expired job listings."*

**Duplicates.** Guideline 3, verbatim: *"The job feed must contain unique job listings to avoid duplicate job content in our search results."*

**Boards reposting boards.** Guideline 6, verbatim: *"The job feed must not contain jobs that have been aggregated from other job boards."*

**Enforcement.** LinkedIn conducts "regular audits," serves "one warning for observed non-compliance," and reserves the right to remove any listing "for any reason."

> **How to read guidelines 3, 4 and 6 in copy:** these are rules LinkedIn had to write. A platform does not forbid stale listings, duplicate listings, and re-aggregated listings unless stale, duplicate, and re-aggregated listings keep arriving. The rulebook is the evidence.

### The architecture itself

That aggregators sit *downstream* of company hiring systems — crawling, normalizing, deduping, and batching — is not contested by anyone. Every published guide to building a job aggregator describes exactly this pipeline. It is safe to describe the mechanism. It is not safe to attach a number to it (see Tier 3).

## Tier 2 — usable only with attribution in the copy

### TalentWorks, "Apply Within 96 Hours" (2017)

<https://talent.works/2017/09/28/getting-ghosted-on-your-job-applications-heres-fix-1-apply-within-96-hours/>

Analysis of roughly 1,600 applications. Found applicants up to **8x** more likely to land an interview when applying within the first 96 hours, with odds declining afterward.

**Rules for use:** write *"one widely cited analysis found…"* — never *"studies show,"* never as our own data, never as a promise. It is nine years old, the sample is modest, and it was published on a company blog. It is real enough to reference and not strong enough to lean on.

## Tier 3 — never publish

Every item below appeared in material handed to us as "citations." Each one traces back to a company that sells a job-monitoring product, self-published, with no disclosed methodology. Using a competitor's unaudited marketing numbers as our facts is precisely the move our own B2B pitch attacks.

| Claim | Where it actually comes from |
|---|---|
| "Aggregator lag is 1.5–3 days; 30% of roles are 5 days late" | FirstPost.io blog. FirstPost sells direct-ATS monitoring. Cites "the measurements above" — no methodology. |
| Applicant-rank table (#1–#5 direct vs #140–#180 via a board digest) | Same FirstPost post. Illustrative, not measured. |
| "Recruiters screen applicants #1–#25 at 3–5x the rate of #100–#200" | Same FirstPost post. No source given. |
| "LinkedIn takes 24 hours / 24–72 hours to show a job" | jobstrack.io, a career-monitoring product, attributing it to LinkedIn's Help Center. **We could not find that statement in LinkedIn's Help Center.** Do not repeat it. |
| "Jobs appear on LinkedIn 18 hours later on average; 36–48 hours for small companies" | Unattributed, surfaced only through the same vendor-blog cluster. |
| "70–80% of jobs are never advertised" | Traces to Bernard Haldane, 1966, describing one city where large employers had agreed to use a local placement service instead of newspapers. No modern basis. |
| "The boards post 20M+ jobs a day" | Misreading of LinkedIn's *total live listings* (~15–22M) as a daily rate. |

One item in the handed-over source list — a `publish.obsidian.md/rolodexter…` link — is a saved transcript of an AI assistant's reply. It is not a source.

## The upshot

We lost nothing by dropping the unsourced numbers. We traded a 1966 statistic and a vendor's latency estimate for **LinkedIn's own signed admission that jobs from company hiring systems may never reach you, and that they reserve the right to charge for the privilege.**

That is a better ad than any statistic, and the citation is a URL on Microsoft's documentation site.

**Pivot the whole story from latency to visibility.** "You were late" invites the reply *"so what, I'll check more often."* "You were never shown the job" has no reply at all.
